Regular fetal movements

Feeling the baby move regularly, especially during the second and third trimesters, is a positive sign of fetal well-being.

Steady weight gain

The mother's weight gain and the baby's growth are monitored throughout pregnancy. Adequate weight gain by the mother and appropriate growth of the fetus are positive signs.

Strong and regular fetal heartbeat

A strong and steady fetal heartbeat can be detected during prenatal visits using a Doppler device or ultrasound. A normal heart rate ranges between 120 and 160 beats per minute.

Normal measurement of the uterus

The healthcare provider measures the size of the uterus during routine check-ups. An appropriate measurement suggests normal fetal growth.

Consistent growth of the baby bump

A gradually expanding belly is a positive sign that the fetus is growing and developing.

Healthy levels of amniotic fluid

The amount of amniotic fluid surrounding the baby is checked during ultrasounds. Sufficient amniotic fluid is necessary for the baby's movement, development, and protection.

Good results from prenatal tests

Prenatal screenings and diagnostic tests, such as ultrasound scans, blood tests, and genetic testing, can provide valuable information about the health and development of the fetus.

Absence of complications or abnormalities

A healthy fetus typically does not show any signs of major abnormalities or complications, such as structural defects or genetic disorders. However, some conditions may not be detectable until later stages of pregnancy or after birth.

Maternal health issues

Certain health conditions in the mother, such as uncontrolled diabetes, high blood pressure, obesity, thyroid disorders, infections,autoimmune diseases, or substance abuse, can have a negative impact on fetal health.

Maternal lifestyle choices

Smoking, alcohol consumption, illicit drug use, or excessive caffeine intake during pregnancy can increase the risk of complications and negatively affect the fetus's development.

Genetic factors

Certain genetic disorders or chromosomal abnormalities, such as Down syndrome, can lead to poor fetal health. These conditions are usually not preventable and occur due to genetic variations or mutations.

Maternal age

Advanced maternal age (usually considered as 35 years or older) is associated with a higher risk of complications and fetal health issues. Older mothers have an increased likelihood of chromosomal abnormalities and conditions like gestational diabetes or high blood pressure.

Infections

Maternal infections during pregnancy, such as rubella, cytomegalovirus, toxoplasmosis, or certain sexually transmitted infections like syphilis, can harm fetal health. These infections can be transmitted to the fetus, leading to developmental problems or birth defects.

Poor prenatal care

Inadequate prenatal care, including a lack of regular check-ups, prenatal vitamins, and necessary medical interventions, can contribute to poor fetal health outcomes. Regular prenatal care is essential for monitoring the health of both the mother and the fetus.

Placental abnormalities

Conditions affecting the placenta, such as placenta previa (when the placenta covers the cervix), placental abruption (premature separation of the placenta), or placental insufficiency (when the placenta doesn't function properly), can affect the oxygen and nutrient supply to the fetus, leading to fetal health problems

Environmental factors

Exposure to environmental toxins, pollutants, radiation, or certain medications during pregnancy can harm fetal health. It's important to follow medical advice and avoid potentially harmful substances.
